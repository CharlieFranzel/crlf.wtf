What is phishing — and how to protect yourself

This page explains common phishing techniques and practical steps you can take to reduce your risk. This is educational material — if you believe you've been targeted, follow the steps below to secure your accounts.

What is phishing?

Phishing is a social-engineering attack where criminals try to trick you into revealing sensitive information (passwords, credit card numbers, 2FA codes) or into installing malware by posing as a trusted person or service.

  • Email phishing — bogus emails with urgent requests or links to fake sites.
  • Spear phishing — targeted messages based on info about you or your contacts.
  • SMiShing / SMS phishing — scams sent via text messages.
  • Voice phishing (vishing) — callers impersonating banks or services.

Common signs of a phishing message

  • Unexpected messages asking for credentials, payment, or personal details.
  • Generic greetings (e.g., “Dear customer”) or small grammar/spelling errors.
  • Sender address doesn’t match the claimed organization (hover to inspect).
  • Urgent threats (“Your account will be closed”) or offers that seem too good to be true.
  • Links that point to unfamiliar domains — hover (desktop) to preview URL.

How to prevent phishing (practical steps)

  • Use unique, strong passwords for every account (use a password manager).
  • Enable multi-factor authentication (MFA/2FA) — preferably an authenticator app or hardware key.
  • Verify sender details — check the email address, not just the display name.
  • Don’t click links in unsolicited messages. Instead, go directly to the site by typing its address or using a bookmark.
  • Inspect links before clicking (hover on desktop, press-and-hold on mobile to preview).
  • Keep software updated — OS, browser, and antivirus/security software.
  • Use a reputable password manager — it prevents filling credentials on fake sites by domain matching.
  • Be cautious with attachments — don’t open unexpected or suspicious files.
  • Use secure networks — avoid entering credentials on public Wi-Fi without a trusted VPN.
Quick tip: If an urgent message pressures you to act now, pause. Contact the organization via a verified channel (official website or phone number) before responding.

If you think you were phished — immediate steps

  • Change the password for the affected account immediately (from a safe device).
  • Revoke active sessions and check account activity for unauthorized access.
  • Enable or reconfigure MFA on the account.
  • If you reused passwords, change them on other sites too.
  • Scan your device for malware and run OS/browser updates.
  • Report the phishing message: forward suspicious email to your organization’s security team or to reporting addresses (e.g., your email provider's phishing report address).
  • Contact your bank if financial info was exposed.
